# Music Rights & Legal Considerations for Venue Streaming

## Why This Matters

Streaming a live performance of copyrighted music without proper licensing can result in:
- **DMCA takedowns** — your stream gets cut mid-show
- **VOD muting** — platforms auto-mute copyrighted audio in recordings
- **Account strikes** — repeated violations can terminate your channel
- **Legal action** — copyright holders can sue for statutory damages ($750-150,000 per infringement)

This isn't theoretical. Venues and artists have been hit with all of the above. Proper licensing protects your venue, your artists, and your stream.

## Understanding Music Licensing for Streams

### The Rights Involved

When you stream a live performance of a song, two separate copyrights are in play:

| Right | What It Covers | Who Owns It | Who Licenses It |
|-------|---------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Musical composition** (publishing) | The underlying song (melody, lyrics, arrangement) | Songwriter / Publisher | PROs (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, GMR) |
| **Sound recording** (master) | A specific recorded performance of the song | Record label / Artist | Record label directly |

**Live performances of cover songs:** You need a license for the **composition** (from PROs). You do NOT need a master recording license because the artist is creating a new performance, not playing back a recording.

**Playing recorded music (DJ sets, walk-in music, intermission playlists):** You need BOTH a composition license (PRO) AND potentially a master use / sync license, which is much harder to obtain for streaming.

### Performing Rights Organizations (PROs)

PROs license the right to publicly perform musical compositions. A "public performance" includes live streaming.

| PRO | Catalog Size | Notable Artists | Website |
|-----|-------------|----------------|---------|
| **ASCAP** | ~18 million works | Beyonce, Stevie Wonder, Lin-Manuel Miranda | ascap.com |
| **BMI** | ~22 million works | Taylor Swift, Mariah Carey, Eminem | bmi.com |
| **SESAC** | ~1.5 million works | Bob Dylan, Neil Diamond, Adele | sesac.com |
| **GMR** | ~100,000 works | Bruce Springsteen, Drake, John Lennon estate | gmrights.com |

**You typically need licenses from all four** to cover the broadest catalog. Most songs are registered with either ASCAP or BMI.

### PRO License Types for Venues

Most venues already hold PRO licenses for live performances in their physical space. **However, streaming is considered a separate "new media" performance right** and requires additional licensing.

| License Type | Covers | Typical Annual Cost | Notes |
|-------------|--------|--------------------|----|
| **Venue live performance license** | Music performed in the physical venue | $500-5,000/year | Most venues already have this |
| **New media / streaming license** | Music performed and streamed online | $500-5,000+/year | Additional license needed for streaming |
| **Special event license** | One-time event (temporary venues) | $50-500/event | For non-permanent venues |

### How to Get Licensed

1. **Check your existing PRO agreements** — Contact ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, and GMR to ask whether your current venue license covers streaming. In many cases, it does not.
2. **Apply for new media licenses** — Each PRO offers online application for streaming/new media rights:
   - ASCAP: "New Media" license category
   - BMI: "Digital/New Media" license
   - SESAC: Contact directly (invitation-based licensing)
   - GMR: Contact directly
3. **Budget $2,000-10,000/year** for comprehensive PRO coverage for streaming (varies by venue size and viewership)

## Platform-Specific Music Policies

### YouTube

- Uses **Content ID** to automatically detect copyrighted music
- Live streams can be interrupted or blocked in real time if Content ID flags a match
- VODs may be muted, monetized by the rights holder, or blocked
- YouTube has its own licensing agreements with some labels — but these don't cover all music
- **For live original music:** Generally safe; false Content ID matches can be disputed
- **For cover songs:** Usually allowed live but VODs may get claimed

### Twitch

- Music detection system (Audible Magic) scans VODs and clips
- Live streams are generally NOT interrupted, but VODs will have flagged sections muted
- Twitch's music licensing is limited — most commercial music is not licensed
- **Twitch Soundtrack** was discontinued; no replacement as of 2026
- **Safest approach on Twitch:** Stream only original music or properly licensed covers

### Facebook Live

- Uses **Rights Manager** to detect copyrighted music
- Live streams can be interrupted or ended if music is detected
- More aggressive than YouTube for live interruption
- **Facebook has no broad music licensing agreement** — use caution

### Vimeo

- No automated content detection (as of 2026)
- You are responsible for ensuring you have rights to all content
- Terms of service require you to have necessary licenses
- Least likely to interrupt a stream, but you bear full legal responsibility

## Specific Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Streaming a Band Playing Their Original Music

**Risk level: Low**

- The band owns (or has the right to perform) their own compositions
- No master recording rights issue (it's a live performance)
- **Get written confirmation** from the artist that they approve streaming of their performance
- **Best practice:** Include streaming permission in your artist/booking agreement

### Scenario 2: Streaming a Band Playing Cover Songs

**Risk level: Moderate**

- You need a **public performance license** for the compositions (PRO licenses)
- No master recording license needed (live performance, not a playback)
- PRO licenses from ASCAP/BMI/SESAC/GMR cover this if you have new media/streaming licenses
- **Content ID may still flag** these on YouTube/Facebook — you can dispute with proof of PRO licensing

### Scenario 3: Playing Recorded Music During Intermission/Walk-In

**Risk level: High**

- Playing back recorded music (Spotify, iTunes, CDs) on a stream triggers BOTH composition and master rights
- PRO licenses cover the composition, but you likely do NOT have a **sync/master license** for the recording
- **Safest approach:** Use royalty-free music libraries for all non-live segments:
  - Epidemic Sound (~$15/month) — large library, stream-safe
  - Artlist (~$10-17/month) — popular with video creators
  - Soundstripe (~$15/month) — curated library
  - YouTube Audio Library (free) — limited but safe for YouTube
  - Free Music Archive (free) — Creative Commons licensed tracks

### Scenario 4: DJ Set / Recorded Music Event

**Risk level: Very High**

- DJ sets involve playing back copyrighted master recordings
- Even with PRO licenses, you likely lack sync/master licenses for each track
- Most platforms will aggressively flag or interrupt DJ set streams
- **Options:** Use a platform that doesn't scan (risky legally), get direct clearance from labels (expensive/complex), or stream only sets using cleared/licensed tracks

### Scenario 5: House of Worship Streaming Hymns and Worship Songs

**Risk level: Low-Moderate**

- **CCLI (Christian Copyright Licensing International)** offers a **Streaming License** specifically for worship services
- CCLI Streaming License: ~$100-500/year depending on congregation size
- Covers most contemporary worship music catalogs
- Traditional hymns in the public domain (generally published before 1928) need no license
- **Get CCLI Streaming Plus** if you also want to display lyrics on stream

## Artist Agreements

### What to Include in Booking Contracts

Add a streaming clause to every artist/booking agreement:

```
STREAMING & RECORDING RIGHTS

Artist grants Venue a non-exclusive license to:
(a) Live stream the Performance via [platforms] during the event
(b) Record the Performance for [archival / VOD replay for X days / promotional clips]
(c) Use artist name, likeness, and approved promotional materials
    in connection with the stream

Artist warrants that they have the right to perform all material
included in their set, including any cover songs.

Venue agrees to:
(a) Maintain appropriate PRO licenses for streaming
(b) Provide [X days] notice before streaming
(c) [Share / not share] stream revenue with Artist at [X%]
(d) Remove VOD content upon Artist request within [X days]
```

**Key points:**
- Always get written permission — verbal agreements are hard to enforce
- Specify whether the artist receives a share of streaming/PPV revenue
- Clarify who owns the recording (venue, artist, or shared)
- Include a takedown provision so artists can request removal

## Privacy Considerations

### Audience Members in Frame

- In most US states, filming in a venue where attendees have a reasonable expectation of being recorded (concerts, events) is legal
- **Post signage** at entrances: "This event is being live streamed. By entering, you consent to being filmed."
- Consider local laws — some jurisdictions have stricter consent requirements
- For private events (corporate, weddings), get explicit consent or avoid filming audience

### GDPR (European Viewers)

If your stream reaches EU viewers:
- You may need a privacy notice on your streaming page
- Avoid clearly identifying individual EU audience members without consent
- Consult with a legal professional if EU viewership is significant

## Key Takeaways

1. **Get PRO streaming licenses** (ASCAP, BMI, SESAC, GMR) — this is non-negotiable for streaming music
2. **Add streaming clauses to all artist contracts** — protect yourself with written permission
3. **Use royalty-free music for non-live segments** (intermission, pre-show, post-show)
4. **Post recording/streaming signage** at your venue entrance
5. **Avoid streaming DJ sets or recorded music playback** unless you have explicit master recording licenses
6. **Houses of worship** should get a CCLI Streaming License
7. **Consult an entertainment attorney** for your specific situation — this guide is informational, not legal advice
