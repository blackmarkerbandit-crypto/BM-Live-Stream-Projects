# EOS — The Six Components: Deep Dive

**Reference:** eosworldwide.com, strety.com, ninety.io  
**Created:** April 2026

---

## Component 1: Vision

> "Get everyone in your organization 100% on the same page with where you're going and how you plan to get there."

### What It Means
Vision is about clarity and alignment. Before you can execute, every person in leadership must agree on the same destination. Most leadership teams *think* they're aligned but are actually operating on different assumptions.

### The Eight Questions (V/TO Framework)
The Vision/Traction Organizer (V/TO) answers eight essential questions that define the business:

1. **Core Values** — What are the 3–5 non-negotiable values that define your culture?
2. **Core Focus** — What is your purpose/cause/passion, and what is your niche?
3. **10-Year Target** — Where do you see the company in 10 years? (Big, specific number or milestone)
4. **Marketing Strategy** — Who is your target customer? What is your unique differentiator?
5. **3-Year Picture** — What does the business look like 3 years from now? Revenue, headcount, capabilities?
6. **1-Year Plan** — What specific, measurable goals must be achieved this year?
7. **Quarterly Rocks** — What are the 3–7 most important priorities for the next 90 days?
8. **Issues List** — What are the biggest obstacles standing in the way?

### Primary Tool
- **V/TO (Vision/Traction Organizer)** — Condenses all eight answers onto two pages; shared with the entire organization

### Key Outcomes
- Leadership team stops operating on different assumptions
- Employees understand how their work connects to the bigger picture
- Strategic planning becomes clear and repeatable

## Component 2: People

> "Surround yourself with great people — people who share your core values and who are in the right seats."

### What It Means
Having the right strategy means nothing with the wrong people. EOS defines "right people" as those who share your core values, and "right seats" as those who are properly positioned in roles they understand, want, and can do.

### Right People: Core Values Alignment
- Defined using the **People Analyzer** tool
- Grade each team member against each Core Value: **+ (yes), +/- (sometimes), - (no)**
- Anyone with consistent minuses on Core Values is a cultural misalignment — regardless of talent

### Right Seats: GWC Assessment
For every role, each person must satisfy all three criteria:

| Letter | Question | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| **G** | Do they "Get it"? | Do they intuitively understand the role and how it fits? |
| **W** | Do they "Want it"? | Do they genuinely want this role and its responsibilities? |
| **C** | Do they have "Capacity"? | Do they have the skills, time, and mental bandwidth to perform well? |

A person who Gets it and Wants it but lacks Capacity can be trained. A person who lacks Want will never perform at the level needed regardless of training.

### The Accountability Chart
- Replaces the traditional org chart
- Maps every major **function** the business performs
- Each seat has **3–7 clear accountabilities** (not tasks — outcomes)
- The Visionary and Integrator sit at the top
- Identifies gaps (seats without owners) and overlaps (too many owners)

### Practical Applications
- **Hiring:** Screen candidates against Core Values first, then GWC
- **Performance Reviews:** Use People Analyzer to structure conversations
- **Firing:** Consistent failure on Core Values or GWC (after coaching) = wrong fit
- **Promotions:** Only promote those who GWC the new seat

## Component 3: Data

> "Manage your business by facts and data, not intuition and emotions."

### What It Means
Most leadership teams make decisions based on feelings, opinions, and incomplete information. Data eliminates the subjectivity and gives everyone a shared, objective view of business health each week.

### The Scorecard
- Tracks **5–15 weekly metrics** that predict business performance
- Focus on **leading indicators** (actions that drive results) not **lagging indicators** (results themselves)
- Example leading indicators: sales calls made, proposals sent, support tickets resolved, client check-ins completed
- Each metric has: a **goal**, an **owner**, and a **weekly status** (on-track or off-track)
- Reviewed in every Level 10 Meeting — takes 5 minutes

### Measurables
- Every individual in the company has **1–3 personal measurables** tied to their seat
- This cascades accountability from the Scorecard down through every level of the organization
- Removes ambiguity: you either hit the number or you didn't

### How to Set Up a Scorecard
1. Identify 5–10 numbers that, if you hit them weekly, would tell you the business is healthy
2. Assign an owner to each metric
3. Set a goal and threshold
4. Review weekly in the Level 10 Meeting
5. Anything consistently off-track drops to the Issues List for IDS

## Component 4: Issues

> "Become great at identifying, discussing, and solving issues — and make them go away forever."

### What It Means
Every business has issues. The difference between great and mediocre companies is how quickly and permanently they solve them. Most teams either avoid issues, talk about symptoms instead of root causes, or solve the same problems repeatedly.

### The Issues List
- A running, living list of problems, opportunities, and decisions
- Maintained at all levels (leadership, departmental, individual)
- Prioritized before each Level 10 Meeting
- Never grows stale — issues get solved, not shelved

### The IDS Process (Identify, Discuss, Solve)

**Step 1 — Identify**
- State what the real, root issue is (not the symptom)
- Ask "Why?" multiple times until you reach the actual cause
- Most teams fail here because they address symptoms rather than root causes

**Step 2 — Discuss**
- Open, honest conversation about the issue
- Stick strictly to what's relevant — no tangents
- Everyone speaks; the loudest voice doesn't win
- Continue until the team fully understands the issue

**Step 3 — Solve**
- Agree on a concrete solution
- Assign it to a specific person
- Set a clear deadline
- Create a To-Do that gets tracked to completion

### Rules for Effective IDS
- No sacred cows — any issue can be discussed
- No deflecting, defending, or politicking
- Solve it once, solve it permanently
- Short-term comfort vs. long-term health: choose long-term

## Component 5: Process

> "Systemize your business so your core processes are identified, documented, simplified, and followed by all."

### What It Means
Processes are how work gets done consistently. Without documented processes, quality depends entirely on who is doing the work. When processes are documented and followed, the business becomes scalable, trainable, and less dependent on any single person.

### Identifying Core Processes
Most businesses have **5–7 core processes** covering:

| Process | What it covers |
|---|---|
| Marketing | How you generate awareness and leads |
| Sales | How you convert prospects to customers |
| Operations/Delivery | How you fulfill your product or service |
| Accounting/Finance | How you manage billing, collections, and reporting |
| Human Resources | How you hire, onboard, develop, and exit people |
| Customer Retention | How you keep clients happy and loyal |

### The 20/80 Rule for Documentation
- Don't try to document everything — that creates 300-page manuals no one reads
- Focus on the **20% of steps that drive 80% of consistent results**
- Each process should be simple enough that any qualified person can follow it
- Think: key steps, decision points, and handoffs — nothing more

### The 3-Step Process Documenter (EOS Tool)
1. **Name the process** (e.g., "Client Onboarding Process")
2. **List the major steps** (5–10 high-level actions)
3. **Assign ownership** — one person owns and maintains each process

### "Followed by All" Requirement
A process only counts if everyone follows it consistently. After documenting, use the **Followed by All checklist** to verify buy-in. Undocumented variation is the enemy of scaling.

### Benefits for Scaling
- New hires can be onboarded faster with less tribal knowledge transfer
- Quality becomes predictable regardless of who does the work
- Leadership can step back knowing the machine runs itself
- Bottlenecks and breakdowns become visible and fixable

## Component 6: Traction

> "Bring discipline and accountability to the organization — establish priorities and keep everyone focused."

### What It Means
This is where vision meets reality. Traction is the execution engine — the tools and rhythms that turn strategic goals into daily action. It's the component most companies lack even when they have great ideas.

### Rocks (90-Day Priorities)
- The **3–7 most important things** the leadership team must accomplish in the next 90 days
- Named after the metaphor: fill a jar with big rocks first, then gravel, then sand — not the other way around
- Each Rock is owned by one person
- Rocks cascade: company Rocks → departmental Rocks → individual Rocks
- Reviewed weekly in Level 10 Meetings (on-track / off-track only)

### Rules for Setting Rocks
- Rocks must be **specific and measurable** — not vague themes
- A Rock is not a task; it's a significant milestone or deliverable
- 3–7 is intentional — if everything is a priority, nothing is
- Rocks are set at Quarterly Planning Sessions and reviewed at the next Quarter's session

### The Meeting Pulse
EOS creates a structured **rhythm of meetings** that keeps everyone aligned and accountable:

| Meeting Type | Frequency | Duration | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| Level 10 Meeting | Weekly | 90 minutes | Team alignment, issue resolution, Rock check-in |
| Quarterly Planning | Every 90 days | Full day (off-site preferred) | Set new Rocks, review V/TO, solve big issues |
| Annual Planning | Once per year | 2 full days | Update vision, 3-year picture, 1-year plan |
| Daily Huddle | Daily (optional) | 10–15 minutes | Quick status update at department level |

The Meeting Pulse is non-negotiable. Skipping it is skipping accountability.
