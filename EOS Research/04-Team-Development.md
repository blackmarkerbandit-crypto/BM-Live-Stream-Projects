## The People Component: Foundation

In EOS, the People component is often where companies struggle most and gain the most. The core principle is deceptively simple:

> **Right People in the Right Seats**

No strategy, process, or tool compensates for a team that isn't aligned on values or properly positioned in their roles.

---

## The Two Dimensions of Every People Decision

### Dimension 1: Right People (Cultural Fit — Core Values)

Right people share your Core Values. They fit your culture naturally. You enjoy working with them. They reinforce the behaviors and standards you want to set for the organization.

Wrong people — regardless of talent or output — erode culture, create friction, and undermine the leadership team's credibility.

**The test:** Run every team member through the **People Analyzer**. Grade them on each Core Value. Consistent minuses on Core Values signal a cultural misfit, and no amount of coaching changes values.

### Dimension 2: Right Seats (Role Fit — GWC)

Right seat means the person Understands their role intuitively, Wants to do it genuinely, and has the Capacity to perform at the required level.

**The most common people failures:**
- Talented people placed in wrong roles (mismatch of Capacity or Want)
- Loyal long-tenured employees who've grown past their peak contribution (GWC declined as the company scaled)
- New hires screened on skills alone without checking values fit

## The Visionary and the Integrator

The two most important roles in any EOS company are defined not by title, but by natural strength and orientation.

### The Visionary
| Trait | Description |
|---|---|
| **Orientation** | Future, ideas, big picture |
| **Strengths** | Creativity, inspiration, culture-building, external relationships |
| **Thinking style** | Emotional, intuitive, possibility-driven |
| **Risk profile** | Comfortable with risk and uncertainty |
| **Typical roles** | Founder, CEO, Chief Visionary Officer |
| **Common struggles** | Details, follow-through, consistency, managing day-to-day operations |

The Visionary answers: **"Where are we going?"**

They generate ideas constantly (often too many), build culture through passion, bring energy to the organization, and maintain relationships with key clients and partners. They are essential — and they are also the primary bottleneck if not properly paired.

### The Integrator
| Trait | Description |
|---|---|
| **Orientation** | Present, execution, accountability |
| **Strengths** | Coordination, conflict resolution, structure, follow-through |
| **Thinking style** | Logical, systematic, detail-oriented |
| **Risk profile** | Calculated, prefers clear data and process |
| **Typical roles** | President, COO, General Manager, Fractional Integrator |
| **Common struggles** | Generating new ideas, inspiring, big-picture thinking |

The Integrator answers: **"How do we get there?"**

They manage the three major business functions (Sales/Marketing, Operations, Finance), resolve issues within the leadership team, and keep the organization executing on the Visionary's ideas. The Integrator is the "glue" — they make the whole machine work.

### The Visionary-Integrator Dynamic

This is the most powerful and most challenging relationship in any EOS company. Research shows companies with strong V-I partnerships grow **3x faster** than those without.

**How it works well:**
- Visionary brings the "what" — Integrator handles the "how"
- They have mutual respect and distinct lanes
- The Visionary stops trying to manage operations; the Integrator stops trying to generate strategy
- They communicate through a structured weekly V-I meeting

**How it breaks down:**
- Visionary overwhelms the Integrator with too many ideas and no prioritization
- Integrator becomes a bottleneck by over-controlling execution
- Both overlap into each other's lanes without clear boundaries
- The Integrator role is left vacant (common in early-stage companies — and the #1 reason EOS stalls)

**Finding an Integrator:**
- Use the Crystallizer Assessment to determine whether existing leaders lean V or I
- Search for Integrator candidates using the same People Analyzer and GWC criteria
- Consider a **Fractional Integrator** if you're not ready for a full-time hire

## Building the Accountability Chart

The Accountability Chart is not just an org chart. It's a strategic alignment tool.

### How to Build It

**Step 1: Start with major functions (blank chart)**
List the 5–8 major functions the business must perform to succeed, regardless of who currently does them:
- Vision/Leadership
- Sales
- Marketing
- Operations
- Finance
- HR/People
- Customer Success

**Step 2: Group into seats**
Combine related functions under one seat (role). Ask: "What single outcome is this seat accountable for?"

**Step 3: Define 3–7 accountabilities per seat**
These are outcomes, not tasks. Example for a Sales seat:
- Revenue targets achieved
- Sales process followed and documented
- Pipeline maintained and accurate
- Team hitting individual quotas
- New client relationships established

**Step 4: Place names**
- One name per seat (if possible)
- A person can be in 2–3 seats temporarily but this is a flag, not a goal
- If a seat has no name: it's a gap — a hiring or promotion priority
- If a seat has multiple names: it's overlap — an accountability confusion risk

**Step 5: Review quarterly**
- Has the structure changed?
- Are there seats that need to be split?
- Are there people who've outgrown or need to vacate seats?

## Developing People Within EOS

### Core Values as the Culture Operating System
Core Values are not a mission statement on the wall. They are behavioral standards that define how the team operates day-to-day.

**How to activate Core Values:**
- Define 2–3 **behavioral examples** for each value so they're observable, not abstract
- Hire and fire against them — say "we're not renewing based on [Core Value] fit" out loud
- Celebrate team members who embody them publicly
- Reference them in decisions: "Does this hire reflect our Core Value of X?"

### The 3-Strike Rule (for People Decisions)
When someone is not in the Right Seat or is failing on Core Values:
1. **Strike 1:** Clarify expectations clearly — make sure they know what's required
2. **Strike 2:** Discuss the gap directly — have the hard conversation
3. **Strike 3:** Make the people decision — move them to a better-fitting seat or out of the organization

Most managers wait too long at Strike 1 or skip to firing without giving fair warning. EOS structures this process to be fair, honest, and decisive.

### 1-on-1 Meetings
Not a formal EOS tool, but essential for people development. Leaders should meet 1-on-1 with each direct report regularly to:
- Discuss issues in their seat
- Apply People Analyzer and GWC feedback
- Develop the person's skills and address gaps
- Build trust and open communication

### Cascading Rocks
Individual development in EOS happens through **cascading Rocks**:
- Company sets 3–7 Rocks at the quarterly session
- Each leader translates those into their department Rocks
- Each individual contributor gets their own Rocks tied to department Rocks
- This creates a direct line of sight from daily priorities to company vision

## Hiring for EOS Companies

### The EOS Hiring Framework
1. **Define the seat first** — Accountability Chart and 3–7 accountabilities must exist before you post a job
2. **Screen for Core Values first** — Before any skills conversation, assess cultural fit
3. **Apply GWC** — Will this person intuitively Get, genuinely Want, and actually have the Capacity for this specific role?
4. **Use behavioral interview questions tied to Core Values** — Ask for examples of past behavior, not hypotheticals
5. **Test for role-specific skills** — Only after values and GWC have been evaluated

### What EOS Companies Look for in Interviews
- "Tell me about a time you [embodied Core Value X]" — evaluates values fit
- "What did you like most/least about your last role?" — evaluates Want
- "Walk me through how you handled [scenario relevant to the seat]" — evaluates Get It and Capacity

### Red Flags to Watch For
- Candidates who criticize every past employer
- People who can't articulate what they're accountable for vs. what they just contribute to
- Anyone who hedges on wanting this specific type of work ("it's fine, I can do it")

## Team Health: The Foundation of a Healthy Leadership Team

A healthy leadership team in EOS has five characteristics:
1. **Trust** — Vulnerability-based trust; people say what they actually think
2. **Conflict** — Productive debate is safe; no passive-aggressive behavior or artificial harmony
3. **Commitment** — Decisions are made and everyone commits, even those who disagreed
4. **Accountability** — Peers hold each other accountable without the leader needing to intervene
5. **Results** — The team prioritizes collective results over individual ego or territory

EOS does not explicitly use Patrick Lencioni's language, but the framework aligns — and many EOS teams pair EOS with *The Five Dysfunctions of a Team* for the leadership health dimension.

### Signs of an Unhealthy Leadership Team
- Issues get raised in the hallway but not in Level 10 Meetings
- Leaders agree in the room and undermine decisions outside it
- People protect their departments instead of helping each other succeed
- The Visionary or Integrator dominates all decisions — others disengage
- Consistent 90-day Rock misses with no honest accountability conversation

### The Annual Team Health Assessment
During the 2-Day Annual Planning session, dedicate the first half of Day 1 to team health. Use an open conversation format:
- What's working in how we operate as a leadership team?
- What's not working?
- What is one thing each person can do differently to make us stronger?
- Are there unresolved issues between team members that need to surface?

This is not a feel-good exercise. It's maintenance that prevents larger failures down the road.
