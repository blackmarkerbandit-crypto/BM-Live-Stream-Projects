# Retainer Client Acquisition — Research & Playbook
## How BMB Finds, Pitches, and Closes Monthly Retainer Clients

**Date:** 2026-04-06  
**Context:** Black Marker Media — streaming production + platform services + BlackMarker.tv  
**Goal:** Land recurring monthly retainer clients across all target verticals

---

## PART 1 — WHO TO TARGET (Client Verticals & Their Pain Points)

Understanding why each client type needs BMB — and what objections they'll raise — is the foundation of every pitch.

---

### Vertical 1: AV Companies (like IDVES)

**Why they need BMB:**
AV companies handle physical event production — lighting, staging, sound, screens. Most don't have an in-house live streaming capability, and when clients ask for it, they either turn down the work or scramble to subcontract it at the last minute. BMB becomes their streaming department without them having to hire one.

**The relationship model:** White-label subcontractor / preferred streaming partner
- IDVES client hires IDVES for the AV
- IDVES brings in BMB for the streaming component
- IDVES marks up BMB's rate or takes a referral fee
- The end client sees a seamless experience

**Their pain points:**
- Losing event revenue when clients ask for streaming they can't provide
- Scrambling to find reliable streaming crew last-minute
- Quality inconsistency when they use random subcontractors
- No ongoing streaming partner they can trust and rely on

**The retainer pitch angle:**
*"We become your dedicated streaming department. You get a reliable partner you can quote into every event. We get a guaranteed volume of work. One agreement, no scrambling."*

**Retainer structure for AV company partners:**
- Preferred partner agreement: first-right-of-refusal on all streaming requests
- Committed minimum: X events per month guaranteed, priced at a bulk rate
- Referral fee model: 10–20% of BMB's invoice for referred-only bookings
- White-label model: BMB operates under AV company brand, higher margin for AV company

**Market size:** The AV industry is a $10B+ market in the US. Thousands of regional AV companies handle corporate events, conferences, and live productions — the vast majority without dedicated streaming infrastructure.

---

### Vertical 2: Churches and Houses of Worship

**The market:**
- **91% of churches now live stream** worship services (Pushpay 2024 State of Church Technology)
- **62% say it's vital to their future** — up 42% from prior year
- **27% of American adults** watch church services online every week
- Professional full-service streaming: **$1,200–$6,000/month** from US providers
- BMB can compete at **$1,500–$3,000/month** with superior local service

**Why they need BMB:**
Most churches that stream are doing it badly — phone cameras, bad audio, cutting in and out. They know it's important but don't have the technical expertise to do it well. The ones that do it well are paying enterprise prices ($2,500–$6,000/month) from remote providers who've never set foot in the building.

**Their pain points:**
- Poor audio quality destroying the online experience
- Inconsistent streaming (drops, freezes, restarts)
- No one on staff who understands the tech
- Overpaying for remote-managed solutions with no local presence
- Guilt about not reaching their homebound, ill, or traveling congregation members

**The retainer pitch angle:**
*"We handle everything — setup, streaming, quality monitoring — so your staff can focus on the service, not the tech. One monthly fee. No surprises."*

**Decision makers:** Senior Pastor, Executive Pastor, Communications Director, Tech Ministry Leader  
**Best entry point:** Offer a free test stream of one service. If they see the quality difference, the pitch closes itself.

**Objection: "We already have someone doing it."**
Response: *"That's great. How's the quality? Are you happy with what your online congregation is experiencing? I'd love to show you what professional-grade looks like — no commitment."*

---

### Vertical 3: Music Venues

**Why they need BMB:**
Local and regional music venues are looking for new revenue streams post-pandemic. Live streaming shows to audiences who can't attend — whether that's due to distance, ticket sellout, or accessibility — extends their reach and revenue. The venues that are doing it are generating ticket sales, tip revenue, and merchandise sales from online viewers.

**Their pain points:**
- No technical crew available for streaming
- Don't know how to monetize a live stream
- Worried about sound/video quality reflecting poorly on the artist
- No audience to stream to (this is where BlackMarker.tv comes in)

**The retainer pitch angle:**
*"We stream your shows on a monthly plan so you stop thinking about it as a per-show cost and start thinking about it as a fixed infrastructure investment — like your sound system lease."*

**Unique BMB angle:** BlackMarker.tv as the distribution network. Venues streaming through BMB get placement on the BlackMarker.tv network — built-in audience, no extra cost. This is a differentiator no pure-production company can offer.

**Retainer structure:**
- Monthly fee covers X shows per month
- Multi-platform distribution included (YouTube, Facebook, BlackMarker.tv, custom RTMP)
- Archive/VOD delivery included
- PPV option available for special events

---

### Vertical 4: Sports Leagues (Youth, Amateur, Regional)

**Why they need BMB:**
Parents, family members, and fans who can't attend games want to watch. Youth sports leagues in particular have huge demand from grandparents, out-of-town family, and traveling parents. Most leagues have no streaming solution at all — or they're using one parent with a phone.

**Their pain points:**
- Parents complain constantly about not being able to watch remotely
- No technical staff for streaming
- Privacy/child safety concerns about public streaming (solution: private or password-protected streams)
- Budget committees that are cautious about new expenses

**The retainer pitch angle:**
*"We handle weekly game streaming on a monthly plan. You tell families it's included in the season fee — or you charge a small subscription. Either way, the complaints about not being able to watch go away."*

**Revenue model for the league:**
- BMB charges league $1,500–$3,000/month retainer
- League can recoup by adding $50–$100/season family subscription fee
- At 200 families × $75 = $15,000/season from a $6,000 service — clear ROI for the league

**Decision makers:** League Commissioner, Athletic Director, Board President

---

### Vertical 5: Corporate Clients (Webinars, Town Halls, Internal Events)

**Why they need BMB:**
Companies with distributed teams need professional-quality internal streaming for town halls, training, product launches, and all-hands meetings. The ones doing it through Zoom or Teams alone are leaving production quality on the table. The ones hiring enterprise AV vendors are overpaying.

**Their pain points:**
- Zoom calls look and feel amateur for important company-wide moments
- No in-house production capability
- One-off event vendors are expensive and unreliable
- Need someone who understands their brand and recurring format

**The retainer pitch angle:**
*"You have a town hall every quarter, product training monthly, and leadership updates weekly. Instead of scrambling to book someone each time, you have us on retainer — we know your brand, your setup, your team."*

**Target size:** Companies with 50–500 employees, distributed teams, active internal communications programs.

---

### Vertical 6: Independent Media / Podcast Networks

**Why they need BMB:**
Podcasters and independent media companies who want to do video podcasts, live recordings, and streaming shows need both the production capability and a platform to distribute on. Most are choosing between bad DIY setups or expensive studio time.

**The retainer pitch angle:**
*"We produce your show, handle the stream, and put it on BlackMarker.tv — so you get professional quality without owning any equipment, and built-in distribution without fighting for algorithm attention."*

**Unique BMB angle:** Again, BlackMarker.tv as a platform differentiator. A podcast network on BlackMarker.tv gets production + platform + marketing in one relationship.

## PART 2 — MARKETING CHANNELS TO REACH RETAINER CLIENTS

### Channel 1: LinkedIn (Highest ROI for B2B)

**Why LinkedIn:**
- 74% of B2B marketers say it's their most effective channel
- 46 million B2B decision-makers active on the platform
- Video content gets 3x more engagement than text posts
- Personal profiles get 5x more engagement than company pages

**Tactics that work:**
1. **Use your personal profile, not the company page.** People hire people, not logos.
2. **Post weekly behind-the-scenes content** — setup photos, event day clips, quality comparisons (bad DIY vs. professional). These attract exactly the right audience.
3. **Short case study videos** — 60–90 seconds showing a before/after or a specific result. "We added streaming to this venue. Here's what happened."
4. **Direct outreach sequence** (see Part 3 for scripts):
   - Connect with decision makers in target verticals
   - Wait 2–3 days, then send a short personalized message
   - No pitch in the first message. Just a relevant observation or question.
   - Follow up once after 5–7 days with the actual value offer

**LinkedIn search filters to find targets:**
- Title: "Worship Pastor," "Communications Director," "Athletic Director," "Event Manager," "AV Director," "Executive Director"
- Location: Your metro area + surrounding region
- Industry: Religious Institutions, Sports, Entertainment, Events Services

---

### Channel 2: Cold Email (Scaleable and Cost-Effective)

**Proven performance benchmarks:**
- Target reply rate: 15%+
- Target conversion to meeting: 1–5%
- Follow-ups boost reply rates by 50%+ — most people give up after 1 email
- Optimal sequence: Initial email → follow-up Day 4 → follow-up Day 8 → break

**The message structure that converts:**
- **Subject line:** 3–5 words, lowercase, specific to their world
  - "church streaming quality" / "venue streaming setup" / "game streaming for [League Name]"
- **Opening line:** Reference something specific about them (their venue, their event, their team)
- **Body:** Lead with a solved problem, not your features
- **CTA:** One simple ask — not "buy this," but "open to a 15-minute call?"

**What kills cold email:** Generic blasts. "We offer live streaming services" sent to 500 people. This gets ignored. Personalization + specificity at the opening is the entire game.

---

### Channel 3: Local Networking and Industry Events

**84% of B2B decision-makers say their buying process starts with a referral.** This is the single most important number in the entire research.

**Local networking tactics:**
- Chamber of Commerce — this is where local business owners go. Join and show up.
- Local AV/events industry meetups — where AV companies and production people gather
- Church communications conferences — these exist and are full of decision-makers actively looking for solutions
- Local sports association meetings — youth sports boards are accessible and approachable
- Music industry local events — venue owner meetups, local promoter networks

**The networking pitch (in person):**
Don't lead with what you do. Lead with a question: *"What does your streaming situation look like right now?"* or *"Are you guys doing anything online for your events?"* Let them describe the problem, then describe the solution.

---

### Channel 4: Referral Program (Turn Existing Clients Into Sales People)

**Referral conversion rates are 3–5x higher than cold outreach.**

**Build a formal referral program:**
- Define the offer clearly: "For every client you refer who signs a retainer, you get [one free month of service / $X cash / a percentage of the contract]"
- Make it easy: Give existing clients a one-pager they can forward
- Follow up consistently: Most referrals aren't given immediately — they come when a conversation happens organically

**For IDVES specifically:**
If IDVES refers a client that signs a retainer, they get:
- Option A: A referral fee (10–15% of first 3 months)
- Option B: A discount on their own retainer rate
- Option C: A revenue share on the project
This converts a client relationship into a sales partnership.

---

### Channel 5: Google Local SEO + Google Business Profile

**Most church, venue, and sports league decision-makers search Google when they have a problem.** Searches like "live streaming services near me," "church streaming setup [city]," "event streaming company [city]" happen constantly.

**What to optimize:**
1. Google Business Profile — fill it out completely, add photos of real work, collect reviews from every client
2. Website service pages targeting local search terms ("live streaming services [city]," "church streaming [city]," "event production [city]")
3. Case study blog posts — "How we helped [type of client] in [city] go from zero to professional streaming" — these rank for specific searches

**Timeline:** SEO takes 3–6 months to produce results but compounds indefinitely. Start now.

---

### Channel 6: Direct Outreach to AV Companies (Partnership Focus)

For AV companies specifically, the outreach is a partnership pitch, not a client pitch.

**Target list building:**
- Search "AV company [your city]" and "audio visual production [your region]"
- Build a list of 20–30 regional AV companies who don't appear to have in-house streaming
- Check their websites — if they don't list "live streaming" as a service, they're a target

**The approach:** Don't cold email. Call or show up. AV companies respect direct, professional people who know the industry. A call works better than email for this audience.

## PART 3 — THE SALES PROCESS: FROM FIRST CONTACT TO SIGNED RETAINER

### Step 1: The First Contact (Warm Up, Don't Pitch)

**For cold outreach (email or LinkedIn):**
Goal of the first message is ONE thing: get a conversation. Not to sell. Not to explain everything. Just to open a door.

**Email template — Church:**
```
Subject: [Church Name] streaming

Hi [Name],

Noticed [Church Name] is doing services online — wanted to reach out because 
we work with several local churches on their streaming setup.

Quick question: are you happy with the quality and reliability of what 
you're putting out right now, or is that something you've been wanting 
to improve?

No pitch — just curious if it's a pain point.

[Your name]
Black Marker Media
```

**Email template — AV Company:**
```
Subject: streaming partnership

Hi [Name],

[Company] does great work on the production side. I wanted to reach out 
because we specialize in live streaming for exactly the kind of events 
you're handling — and we work with a few AV companies in the region as 
their streaming partner when clients need it.

Worth a 15-minute call to see if there's a fit?

[Your name]
Black Marker Media
```

**Email template — Sports League:**
```
Subject: [League Name] game streaming

Hi [Name],

We handle game streaming for [type of league] in [region] and wanted to 
reach out — a lot of leagues are getting requests from families who want 
to watch remotely but don't have a solution yet.

Is that something that's come up for [League Name]?

[Your name]
Black Marker Media
```

---

### Step 2: The Discovery Call (Understand Before You Pitch)

This is the most important step most people skip. Before pitching anything, ask:

1. *"What does your streaming situation look like right now?"*
2. *"What's working? What isn't?"*
3. *"Who's the person responsible for making this happen on your end?"*
4. *"What would 'great' look like for you — what problem would be solved?"*
5. *"What's the biggest obstacle to getting there?"*

**Listen for:** budget signals, technical frustration, staffing gaps, past bad experiences, what they've already tried.

Only after understanding their situation do you describe what BMB does — and you frame it in their language, not yours.

---

### Step 3: The Retainer Pitch (Sell the Outcome, Not the Hours)

**Don't say:** "We charge $2,000/month and you get X hours and Y deliverables."

**Do say:** "Here's what your life looks like if we work together..."

**Frame the retainer in terms of outcomes:**
- "Every Sunday your stream goes out professionally — no more hoping it works."
- "Every game is archived and families can watch from anywhere."
- "Every event you quote, you can add streaming — and you know it'll be handled."

**The comparison anchor:**
Show them what they're paying today vs. what a retainer costs.

*"If you're booking us per event at $800 three times a month, that's $2,400 with no consistency and no guaranteed availability. For $2,000/month on retainer, you get four slots, guaranteed crew, priority scheduling, and one invoice. You actually save money while getting more."*

**The risk reversal:**
Offer a trial period — one month, one event, one service — before asking for a longer commitment. This removes the fear of commitment without reducing the value.

---

### Step 4: Handling Objections

**Objection 1: "We can't afford a monthly commitment."**
> *"Totally understand. Can I ask — what are you spending per event right now when you do book streaming? [Listen.] So if you're doing 2–3 of those a month, you're already spending the retainer amount but without the consistency or priority access. What if we structured a smaller pilot — just one month — so you can see the value before committing?"*

**Objection 2: "We already have someone doing it."**
> *"That's great. Are you happy with the quality? Is it consistent? Does it ever feel like a scramble to get it set up? [Listen.] I'm not asking you to replace anyone right now — I'd just love to do one side-by-side so you have a baseline for comparison."*

**Objection 3: "We need to think about it / talk to others."**
> *"Absolutely. What specific concerns do you need to work through? [Listen.] I want to make sure I've given you everything you need to make a decision — what would make this an easy yes?"*

**Objection 4: "Can't you just do it per event?"**
> *"We can — and for one-off events we do. But the retainer exists because our clients who do recurring events told us they wanted reliability and simplicity. One price, no surprises, guaranteed availability. For anyone with a regular streaming need, it's always cheaper and less stressful than per-event. But if you're not sure about the volume yet, let's do a per-event booking first and revisit the retainer after you've seen what we can do."*

---

### Step 5: The Proposal (What to Include)

A retainer proposal for BMB should be 1–2 pages max. No one reads 10-page documents. Here's the structure:

**Section 1: What We Heard (show you listened)**
One paragraph reflecting their specific situation back to them. This builds trust and proves the proposal is not generic.

**Section 2: What We'll Do (deliverables)**
Specific, concrete, round numbers. Not vague promises.
- "2 live streaming events per month"
- "Multi-platform distribution to YouTube + Facebook + BlackMarker.tv"
- "Archived recording delivered within 24 hours"
- "Monthly performance summary"

**Section 3: What's NOT Included (protect yourself)**
- Travel outside X radius
- Events over X hours
- Additional platforms beyond included set
- Rush bookings with less than X days notice

**Section 4: Investment**
One clear monthly price. Include both monthly and annual options (annual at ~10–15% discount creates urgency to commit).

| Option | Monthly | Annual |
|---|---|---|
| Month-to-month | $2,000/mo | — |
| Annual commitment | — | $20,400/yr ($1,700/mo) |

**Section 5: How to Get Started**
Clear next step. Sign here. Pay first month. Schedule onboarding call.

---

### Step 6: After the Close (Retention Beats Acquisition)

Keeping a client costs 5–7x less than finding a new one. Studios that implement formal monthly reporting see **renewal rates climb 10–20 percentage points**.

**Minimum ongoing client touches:**
- Monthly performance summary (even if simple — streams delivered, viewer counts, quality notes)
- Quarterly check-in call — *"How are things going? Anything you'd like to change?"*
- Proactive communication about upcoming events — never make them chase you
- Occasional unexpected value add — a clip highlight, a social media post, a technical improvement

## PART 4 — THE AV COMPANY PARTNERSHIP STRATEGY (IDVES Model)

This is a separate and highly leveraged approach. Rather than finding individual clients one by one, partnering with AV companies gives BMB access to their entire client roster.

### The Partnership Pitch to an AV Company

**Frame it as:** You're making them more money, not competing with them.

*"You already handle the physical production for these clients. When streaming comes up, you either turn it down or scramble. We solve that — we're your streaming department. You quote it into your events, mark it up, and we handle delivery. You make more money per client with zero extra staff."*

### Two Partnership Models to Offer

**Model A: Referral Agreement**
- AV company refers clients to BMB for streaming
- BMB pays AV company 10–15% of the invoice for each referred client
- Clean, simple, no coordination required
- Best for: AV companies that want passive income without involvement

**Model B: Subcontractor / White-Label Agreement**
- BMB operates as part of the AV company's crew — no BMB branding on site
- AV company invoices the client directly, marks up BMB's rate
- BMB handles streaming delivery seamlessly
- Best for: AV companies that want to control the client relationship and appear full-service

**Key elements of the partnership agreement:**
1. First-right-of-refusal: AV company comes to BMB before anyone else for streaming needs
2. Committed volume: If AV company guarantees X events per month, BMB provides a discounted bulk rate
3. Exclusivity clause (optional): BMB doesn't directly approach AV company's existing client list
4. Referral fee structure: Clear, documented, paid monthly
5. Quality standards: Agreed upon so both parties' reputations are protected

### Why AV Companies Are BMB's Best Sales Channel

One signed AV partner can deliver 5–20 streaming engagements per year without BMB doing any additional sales. Multiply by 3–5 AV partners in the region and BMB has a de facto sales force of established companies with existing client relationships.

**Target: 3–5 AV company partnerships in Year 1**
